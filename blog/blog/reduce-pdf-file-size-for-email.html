How to Reduce PDF File Size for Email Attachments

Published July 2026

"Message too large" is one of the most common email errors, and PDFs are usually the culprit. Most email providers cap attachments somewhere between 20-25MB, and a PDF with a handful of scanned pages or embedded images can easily blow past that. Here's why it happens and how to fix it in under a minute.

Why PDFs get so large in the first place

A PDF isn't really "one file type" — it's a container that can hold several different kinds of content, and each one affects size differently:

  • Scanned pages — a scanned document is really just a photo of each page, and photos are the single biggest driver of file size. A 10-page scan at high resolution can easily reach 20-30MB on its own.
  • Embedded high-resolution images — photos, screenshots, or graphics pasted into a document at full camera resolution, when the PDF only needs to display them at screen size.
  • Duplicate or unused fonts/data — some PDF creation tools embed extra data that a smaller, cleaner export wouldn't include.

Common email attachment limits (so you know your target)

  • Gmail: 25MB per attachment
  • Outlook / Microsoft 365: 20MB per attachment (varies by organization)
  • Yahoo Mail: 25MB per attachment

If your file is close to or over these limits, compression is usually the fastest fix — much quicker than re-exporting or re-scanning the original document.

What to do if compression isn't enough

Some files (especially scans of many pages) are large enough that compression alone won't get them under an email limit. In that case:

  • Split the file — use a split tool to send the document in two or three smaller parts instead of one large attachment
  • Use a cloud link instead of an attachment — upload the file to Google Drive or Dropbox and share a link, which sidesteps email size limits entirely
